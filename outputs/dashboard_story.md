# Dashboard Story: Retail Executive Performance Review

**Audience:** Leadership Team
**Period:** January 2024 – December 2025
**Purpose:** Monitor sales performance, profitability, customer segments, category health, shipping efficiency, discount impact, and return patterns to identify opportunities and risks.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The business generated **$217M in total sales** and **$33.3M in profit** (15.3% margin) over the two-year period. Sales grew modestly from $106.2M in 2024 to $110.8M in 2025 — a healthy 4.3% year-over-year increase. Technology dominates as the revenue and profit engine, contributing 71% of total profit despite being one of three categories. However, two structural risks are visible in the data: **aggressive discounting is destroying margin**, and **Furniture is a volume leader with dangerously thin profitability**.

The story the dashboard tells is not one of crisis — the business is growing and profitable — but one of **profit quality**. Leadership needs to decide whether to defend current volume with discounts, or shift strategy toward protecting and growing margin.

---

## 2. What Is Performing Well

### Technology is the growth engine
Technology generated $153.9M in sales and $28M in profit (18.2% margin) — more than triple the profit of Furniture and Office Supplies combined. Sub-categories Copiers, Accessories, Phones, and Machines all deliver margins above 18%. This category is both the largest revenue contributor and the most profitable. Investment here yields the highest return.

### South region leads all regions
The South region contributed $64.7M in sales and $10M in profit — the highest of all four regions. It outperforms the next-best region (North) by nearly $10M in sales. The South should be studied as a model for what is working from a go-to-market perspective.

### Year-over-year growth is consistent
2025 sales ($110.8M) exceeded 2024 ($106.2M) by 4.3%, and profit grew proportionally ($16.8M vs $16.5M). The business is not stalling. Month-on-month peaks in Q1 and Q3 suggest seasonality patterns that can be planned around.

### Organic channel dominates with healthy returns
Organic campaign channel drives $88.8M in sales (41% of total) at a 15.1% margin, and has the second-lowest return rate (4%). This indicates strong product-market fit for organically acquired customers.

---

## 3. What Is Underperforming

### Furniture margin is critically thin
Furniture generated $51.6M in sales — a substantial volume contribution — but only $3.6M in profit (6.9% margin). Tables and Bookcases are the weakest sub-categories, each delivering just 5.7% margin. At this level, any increase in costs, returns, or discounts pushes these sub-categories into loss. Furniture is consuming resources and shelf space without generating proportionate profit.

### Discounts above 20% are destroying profitability
Orders with discounts above 30% are collectively unprofitable (−5% margin, net loss of $102K). Orders with 21–30% discounts yield just 8% margin. In contrast, undiscounted orders deliver 20.6% margin. There is a clear inverse relationship between discount depth and profit margin. The business is likely using heavy discounts to drive volume in low-performing categories, which compounds the profitability problem.

### East and West regions trail significantly
The East ($48.9M) and West ($48.9M) regions are nearly identical in sales but both trail the South by $15.8M. Neither region is growing as strongly, suggesting either market penetration gaps or go-to-market weaknesses.

### Home Office segment has the highest return rate
Home Office customers return products at 5.7% — significantly higher than Consumer (3.9%) and Corporate (4.0%). This is a product-market fit signal: Home Office customers may be purchasing products that don't meet their needs, or the product descriptions are misaligned with expectations.

---

## 4. What Risks Are Visible

### Discount dependency risk
The negative correlation between discount and profit (−0.27) confirms that discounting is systematically reducing margin. If volume becomes dependent on discounts to sustain it, margin will continue to compress as the business scales. This is a structural risk, not a one-time issue.

### Furniture return rate is the highest of all categories
Furniture has a 7.7% return rate — more than double Technology (3.0%) and significantly above Office Supplies (3.7%). High return rates in a low-margin category are doubly damaging: they erode already thin margins while generating operational costs for reverse logistics and customer service.

### Referral channel has the highest return rate
Referral-acquired customers have a 6% return rate, the highest of any channel. If the business is investing in referral programs, the quality of those referrals needs to be reassessed.

### No Same-Day or First Class shipping advantage on profit
Same Day shipping yields the highest average profit per order ($9,101), but Standard Class ($7,839) follows at significantly lower delivery cost. If Standard Class achieves comparable profitability while costing less to fulfil, over-investment in premium shipping modes for non-urgent orders is a margin leak.

---

## 5. What Opportunities Are Visible

### Grow Technology share of revenue
Technology's 18.2% margin versus Furniture's 6.9% suggests reallocating marketing and sales investment toward Technology sub-categories would improve overall portfolio margin without needing to grow total revenue. Specifically, Copiers (18.0%), Accessories (18.3%), and Phones (18.5%) are the highest-margin sub-categories and have room to scale.

### Reduce or restructure discount strategy
Eliminating discounts above 20% across the board — or restricting them to strategic customer retention cases — could recover several percentage points of margin. The data shows undiscounted orders perform at 20.6% margin vs 8.0% for 21–30% discount orders. Even a partial shift would meaningfully improve profitability.

### Investigate and replicate South region success
The South's outperformance by $10M in sales over the next region suggests something is working — whether it's the sales team, channel mix, customer segment composition, or product mix. Understanding and replicating this model in the East and West could close the regional performance gap.

### Target Corporate segment for discount reduction
Corporate customers represent significant volume ($70.6M) with a 4.0% return rate — healthy. If Corporate customers are also receiving heavy discounts to maintain loyalty, there may be room to reduce discounts for this segment without significant churn risk, improving margin quality.

---

## 6. Recommended Business Actions

| Priority | Action | Expected Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| High | Cap discounts at 20% across all categories | Recover 2–5% margin on discounted orders |
| High | Conduct profitability review for Tables and Bookcases | Decide whether to reprice, reposition, or discontinue |
| Medium | Investigate Furniture return drivers (product quality vs expectations) | Reduce 7.7% return rate, improve margin |
| Medium | Replicate South region go-to-market model in East and West | Close $10–15M revenue gap |
| Medium | Shift marketing investment toward Technology sub-categories | Improve portfolio margin without growing total revenue |
| Low | Review referral channel quality and incentive structure | Reduce 6% return rate from referral-acquired customers |
| Low | Analyse Home Office segment purchase patterns | Address 5.7% return rate — highest of any segment |

## 7. Limitations of the Dashboard

- **No customer lifetime value data:** The dashboard shows transaction-level sales but cannot measure customer retention, repeat purchase rate, or long-term value. A high-volume segment with high churn could look healthy here while actually being at risk.
- **No cost-of-goods data:** Profit is available but cost structure (logistics, manufacturing, overhead) is not broken out. Margin comparisons between categories may not fully reflect true profitability once all cost layers are included.
- **Campaign channel has missing values:** Approximately 1.5% of orders have no campaign channel attributed. These are excluded from channel analysis, which may slightly skew channel performance numbers.
- **Two-year window only:** Trend analysis covers 2024–2025. This is insufficient to distinguish structural trends from cyclical variation. A 3–5 year view would provide stronger signal.
- **Return flag is binary:** The dataset indicates whether a return occurred but not the reason, value recovered, or cost incurred. This limits the depth of return pattern analysis.
- **No inventory or stock data:** Popular sub-categories like Copiers and Accessories may face supply constraints not visible in the sales data.

---

## 8. Suggested Next Analysis

1. **Customer cohort analysis** — Track repeat purchase rates and churn by segment and channel to understand true customer lifetime value
2. **Discount elasticity modelling** — Test whether reducing discounts by 5–10% causes proportionate volume loss or whether demand holds, to quantify the discount reduction opportunity
3. **Furniture deep-dive** — Break down Furniture costs, pricing, and return reasons to determine whether low margin is structural (wrong price point) or operational (returns, logistics)
4. **Regional market penetration study** — Map customer density and market share by state to identify whether East and West underperformance reflects untapped market potential or competitive pressure
5. **Seasonality forecasting** — Use 2-year monthly data to build a seasonal demand model and improve inventory and campaign planning for Q1 and Q3 peaks
